Jobs-to-be-Done Analysis · DentalTechHub

Core Job: "Selecting the right technology solution to address a specific practice need"

Performed by Practice Owners, Office Managers, DSO Executives, and DSO Procurement Specialists — all following a common 12-step process.

Where DentalTechHub Can Create the Most Value

The highest-leverage opportunity sits in Steps 4–8 (Discover → Compare), where the buying process is most fragmented and poorly served today.

Step Level Core Problem
Step 4: Discover 🔴 Very High Fragmented info, biased sources, no central hub
Step 5: Screen 🔴 Very High Manual process, inconsistent criteria, time-consuming
Step 6: Evaluate 🔴 Very High Hidden pricing, outdated info, hard to compare, fake reviews
Step 7: Validate 🟠 High Hard to find references, vendor-controlled trial access
Step 8: Compare 🟠 High No apples-to-apples tools, analysis paralysis
Steps 1–3: Define / Require / Categorise 🟡 Medium Internal process; marketplace can support with education/content
Step 9: Negotiate 🟡 Medium Vendor-buyer relationship; marketplace can provide benchmarks
Steps 10–12: Approve / Implement / Assess 🟢 Lower Happen outside marketplace scope

Job Map: 12 Chronological Steps

# Job Step
1Define the operational or clinical problem requiring a technology solution
2Determine the requirements and constraints for an acceptable solution
3Identify the category of technology that addresses the need
4 🔴Discover available solutions in the market
5 🔴Screen solutions against basic requirements to create a shortlist
6 🔴Evaluate shortlisted solutions in depth (features, pricing, reviews, demos)
7 🟠Validate solution fit through references, trials, or proof-of-concept
8 🟠Compare finalists and make a selection decision
9Negotiate terms, pricing, and contracts with the selected vendor
10Approve the purchase through internal stakeholders or budget holders
11Implement the selected solution into practice operations
12Assess whether the solution delivered expected value post-implementation

Desired Outcomes by Job Step

All outcomes follow the form: "Minimize the likelihood / time / risk / effort of…" — expand each step to read them.

🔴 Step 4 — Discover available solutions in the market (6 outcomes)
  1. Minimize the time it takes to find relevant solutions
  2. Minimize the likelihood of missing strong contenders
  3. Minimize noise from irrelevant or low-quality options
  4. Minimize reliance on biased sources (vendor marketing, paid ads)
  5. Minimize the effort required to compile an initial list
  6. Minimize outdated information about available solutions
🔴 Step 5 — Screen solutions to create a shortlist (5 outcomes)
  1. Minimize time spent evaluating obviously unsuitable options
  2. Minimize the likelihood of eliminating good options prematurely
  3. Minimize the effort required to check basic fit criteria
  4. Minimize inconsistency in how options are screened
  5. Minimize shortlists that are too long or too short
🔴 Step 6 — Evaluate shortlisted solutions in depth (8 outcomes)
  1. Minimize the time required to deeply evaluate each option
  2. Minimize inaccurate or outdated product information
  3. Minimize difficulty comparing features across solutions
  4. Minimize hidden pricing or cost surprises
  5. Minimize reliance on vendor claims without independent validation
  6. Minimize the effort required to schedule and conduct demos
  7. Minimize fake or untrustworthy reviews
  8. Minimize difficulty finding reviews from similar practices
🟠 Step 7 — Validate solution fit (6 outcomes)
  1. Minimize the difficulty of finding relevant reference customers
  2. Minimize the time required to conduct a trial or pilot
  3. Minimize the risk of trials that don't reflect real-world use
  4. Minimize vendor friction in providing trial access
  5. Minimize references that are cherry-picked or unrepresentative
  6. Minimize the effort required to evaluate trial results
🟠 Step 8 — Compare finalists and make a selection decision (6 outcomes)
  1. Minimize difficulty making apples-to-apples comparisons
  2. Minimize analysis paralysis from too many factors
  3. Minimize decisions based on incomplete information
  4. Minimize regret from choosing the wrong solution
  5. Minimize stakeholder disagreement on the final choice
  6. Minimize the time from evaluation completion to decision
Step 1 — Define the operational or clinical problem (5 outcomes)
  1. Minimize ambiguity about what problem needs to be solved
  2. Minimize the likelihood of solving the wrong problem
  3. Minimize time spent debating whether technology is the right answer
  4. Minimize misalignment between stakeholders on the problem definition
  5. Minimize the risk of addressing symptoms rather than root causes
Step 2 — Determine requirements and constraints (6 outcomes)
  1. Minimize requirements that are unrealistic or unnecessary
  2. Minimize overlooked requirements that surface later
  3. Minimize ambiguity about must-haves vs. nice-to-haves
  4. Minimize the effort required to document requirements
  5. Minimize budget constraints discovered late in the process
  6. Minimize technical constraints (integrations, infrastructure) discovered late
Step 3 — Identify the technology category (5 outcomes)
  1. Minimize confusion about what type of solution to look for
  2. Minimize the risk of searching in the wrong category
  3. Minimize time spent learning unfamiliar technology categories
  4. Minimize overlooking emerging categories that could solve the problem better
  5. Minimize jargon and terminology confusion
Step 9 — Negotiate terms, pricing, and contracts (6 outcomes)
  1. Minimize overpaying relative to fair market price
  2. Minimize unfavorable contract terms
  3. Minimize hidden fees discovered after signing
  4. Minimize time spent in negotiation
  5. Minimize lock-in clauses that reduce future flexibility
  6. Minimize the complexity of understanding contract terms
Step 10 — Approve the purchase (5 outcomes)
  1. Minimize delays in getting purchase approved
  2. Minimize rework to justify the decision to stakeholders
  3. Minimize surprises that derail approval
  4. Minimize the effort required to build a business case
  5. Minimize approvers' concerns about the recommendation
Step 11 — Implement the selected solution (6 outcomes)
  1. Minimize the time from purchase to go-live
  2. Minimize disruption to practice operations during implementation
  3. Minimize implementation surprises not anticipated during evaluation
  4. Minimize staff resistance to the new solution
  5. Minimize vendor support gaps during implementation
  6. Minimize training time required for staff proficiency
Step 12 — Assess post-implementation value (5 outcomes)
  1. Minimize the gap between expected and actual value delivered
  2. Minimize difficulty measuring solution impact
  3. Minimize time spent troubleshooting underperformance
  4. Minimize switching costs if solution fails to deliver
  5. Minimize the likelihood of repeating mistakes in future selections

Related Jobs

Before the Core Job (4 jobs)
  1. Staying informed about technology trends in dental
  2. Benchmarking current technology stack against peers
  3. Building internal alignment on technology investment priorities
  4. Securing budget for technology investments
During the Core Job (4 jobs)
  1. Educating oneself on unfamiliar technology categories
  2. Coordinating evaluation activities across stakeholders
  3. Managing vendor communications and follow-ups
  4. Documenting evaluation findings and decisions
After the Core Job (4 jobs)
  1. Managing ongoing vendor relationship
  2. Tracking solution performance and ROI
  3. Planning for upgrades, renewals, or replacements
  4. Sharing learnings with peers about solution performance
Emotional Jobs (what the executor wants to feel)
  1. Feel confident that I'm making an informed decision
  2. Feel protected from making a costly mistake
  3. Feel in control of the process despite complexity
  4. Feel smart and capable navigating technology decisions
  5. Feel secure that I won't be blamed if the solution fails
  6. Feel relieved when the decision is made
  7. Feel proud of selecting a solution that works well
  8. Feel trusted by stakeholders for my recommendation
Social Jobs (how the executor wants to be perceived)
  1. Be perceived by leadership as making sound technology decisions
  2. Be perceived by staff as selecting tools that make their jobs easier
  3. Be perceived by peers as technologically savvy
  4. Be perceived by vendors as a knowledgeable, serious buyer
  5. Be perceived as a good steward of practice resources
  6. Be perceived as someone who does thorough due diligence
